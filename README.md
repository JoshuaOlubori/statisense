## üìå Executive Summary

### The "K-Shaped" Economic Divergence

This report provides a multi-dimensional analysis of Nigeria‚Äôs economic trajectory over the last decade (2015‚Äì2024). The data reveals a phenomenon of **"K-shaped" divergence**: while the financial and digital economies are reaching record heights, the real sector‚Äîmanufacturing, telecommunications, and consumer goods‚Äîis undergoing a structural contraction. This split is primarily driven by three external and internal shocks: aggressive currency devaluation, the removal of the petrol subsidy, and persistent security challenges in the agricultural belt.

### Key Highlights

- **Financial Sector Boom:** The Financial & Insurance sector has decoupled from the broader economy, recording a **29.6% YoY growth in 2024**. This is largely a result of high-interest-rate environments and foreign exchange (FX) revaluation gains.
- **Cost of Living Crisis:** Inflation has transitioned from a chronic issue to an acute emergency. In 2024, **Imported Food inflation reached 41.3%**, signaling a near-total pass-through of exchange rate volatility into the average Nigerian‚Äôs diet.
- **Corporate Profitability Shift:** A massive redistribution of value is underway. While **Zenith Bank** saw profits jump by **153% in 2023**, industrial giants like **MTN Nigeria** and **Nestl√© Nigeria** recorded historical losses, with MTN‚Äôs losses widening by **192% in 2024** due to massive FX-related liabilities.

### Core Insight

Nigeria is no longer a "rising tide that lifts all boats." Policy intervention must shift from headline GDP targeting to **sector-specific stabilization**, particularly focusing on the FX-exposed manufacturing and telecommunications sectors which are the country's primary non-oil employers.

## üìä 1. GDP Analysis: The Shift from Oil to Services

### Overview

The Nigerian economy has undergone a slow but definitive structural shift. For decades, Oil & Gas was the heartbeat of the nation, but the period between 2015 and 2024 shows a sector in terminal struggle, with a **CAGR of -4.4%**. In contrast, the Services sector‚Äîled by Finance and ICT‚Äîhas stepped in to fill the vacuum, though it does not yet provide the same level of foreign exchange liquidity as the oil sector once did.

### üöÄ Top 5 Sectors by Growth (2024)

1. **Financial & Insurance (29.6%):** This sector's growth is "pro-cyclical" to the current crisis. Higher Central Bank policy rates and the revaluation of dollar assets have created a windfall for Tier-1 banks.
2. **Water Supply & Waste Management (8.4%):** A surprising outlier, indicating increased urbanization and gradual privatization of utility services.
3. **Transportation & Storage (6.5%):** A recovery phase following the logistics shocks of 2022-2023, though still hampered by high fuel costs.
4. **Information & Communication (5.4%):** Despite corporate losses in the sector, the _volume_ of digital activity (data usage) continues to grow, making it a "necessity" in the modern Nigerian economy.
5. **Manufacturing - Oil & Gas (4.8%):** A mild recovery in refining and midstream activities, offering a glimmer of hope for domestic energy security.

### üß± Top Contributors to GDP (2024)

## üìâ 2. CPI & Inflation: The Food Security Emergency

### Inflation Dynamics

Nigeria's inflation is no longer just a monetary phenomenon; it is a cost-push crisis. The removal of the fuel subsidy in 2023 and the subsequent unification of the Naira exchange rate acted as a dual shock to the Consumer Price Index (CPI).

### üî• Highest Cost Drivers (2024)

- **Imported Food (+41.3%):** Because Nigeria remains heavily dependent on imported wheat, sugar, and specialty fats, the devaluation of the Naira has directly translated into "imported hunger."
- **General Food (+39.8%):** Driven by domestic logistics costs (diesel) and the displacement of farmers in the North-Central "food belt."
- **Transport (+31.7%):** The permanent reset of fuel prices has increased the "last-mile" cost of every good sold in the country.

### ‚è≥ Cumulative Impact (2015‚Äì2024)

The data suggests that food prices have effectively quintupled (500% increase) over the decade. This has led to a **"hollowing out" of the middle class**, where discretionary spending on **Entertainment**, **Real Estate**, and **Non-essential Services** has collapsed as households reallocate nearly 60-70% of income to food.

## üß≠ 4. Recommendations

### üèõÔ∏è For the Nigerian Economic Advisory Team

1. **Prioritize "Food Sovereignty":** Move beyond "loans for farmers." Focus on **secure transport corridors** and solar-powered cold storage to reduce the 40% post-harvest loss that keeps domestic food inflation high.
2. **FX Intervention for Essential Industry:** Introduce a "Managed Floating Window" specifically for critical manufacturing inputs to prevent the total exit of multinational producers.
3. **Taxing the Windfall:** Consider a temporary "Windfall Tax" on banking revaluation gains to fund critical infrastructure, ensuring the financial boom supports the real sector recovery.

### üíº For Investors

- **Overweight: Tier-1 Banks.** They remain the most efficient hedge against Naira volatility and high-inflation environments.
- **Overweight: Upstream Energy.** Companies like **Seplat** represent a way to play the Nigerian economy with "hard currency" protection.
- **Avoid: Highly Leveraged Consumer Goods.** Until the exchange rate stabilizes, these firms will continue to report "accounting losses" that prevent dividend payouts.
